Estuary

Type:

Web Map

Tags:

estuary; biodiversity

Snippet:

Hudson River Estuary habitats

Desription:

This map depicts significant habitats of the Hudson River Estuary, the tidal portion the Hudson River stretching 153 miles from New York Harbor to the Federal Lock and Dam in Troy. Data shown include: migratory fish runs, significant coastal fish and wildlife habitat areas, submerged aquatic vegetation, and tidal marsh habitat types.


Tidal marshes, submerged aquatic vegetation (SAV), mudflats, and other significant habitats in and along the estuary support a great diversity of plants, fish, and wildlife and contribute to the economic significance of the estuary. More than 200 species of fish are found in the Hudson, including key commercial and recreational species like striped bass, and species of conservation concern like Atlantic and shortnose sturgeon. SAV beds consist of under-water plants that improve water quality in the Hudson and provide essential habitat for invertebrate animals, which feed fish and waterfowl that use the estuary. Tidal wetland habitats play a critical role as nursery grounds for fish and shellfish species, as well as providing nesting sites and migration stops for birds and sources of nutrients for the estuary food web. These wetland systems also help filter pollutants, buffer shoreline properties, and help stabilize the river’s shoreline.

 

Several important fish species depend on travel between ocean and freshwater habitats in their life cycle, such as American eel and river herring. Migratory fish runs highlight stream reaches providing important aquatic habitat connectivity throughout the estuary watershed. Dams and poorly designed and installed culverts can isolate these species from critical habitat, in addition to impacting local flooding and water quality.

 

Significant Coastal Fish and Wildlife Habitats have been designated for protection by the New York State Department of State in accordance with the New York Waterfront Revitalization and Coastal Resources Act and are useful for planning at the local level because they describe the highest quality areas of the Hudson and its tidal tributaries, outlining fish and wildlife values and activities that may have large impacts on the habitats. Additional areas of known importance for rare plants, rare animals, and significant natural communities are shown in the Biodiversity tab.  


Local land-use decisions and management of properties along the Hudson have a vital role in the future of Hudson shorelines and estuarine habitats. Furthermore, global sea level rise will fundamentally affect the estuary in the coming decades. Natural shorelines will potentially allow for the migration of tidal and shoreline habitats as sea level rises and mitigate damage to property and infrastructure.


Note: New York State jurisdictional tidal wetlands (south of the Tappan Zee Bridge) can be identified using the State Environmental Quality Review (SEQR) Environmental Assessment Form Mapper.

Streams

Type:

Web Map

Tags:

watershed; stream; trout; floodplain

Snippet:

Streams and watersheds in the Hudson River Estuary Watershed

Desription:

This map depicts several characteristics of streams and their watersheds, the area of land draining to a stream, river, lake, or other waterbody. Data shown include: HUC10 and HUC12 watershed boundaries and statistics for each on percent impervious surfaces and forest cover, DEC stream classifications and FEMA flood hazard maps.

Watersheds are divided by high points on the land, such as ridges, mountains, and hills, and may be made up of many smaller drainage areas, or subwatersheds. Healthy streams and watersheds with a high percentage of forest cover will recharge groundwater, reduce flood impacts, minimize public infrastructure and water treatment costs, and be more resilient to climate change, in addition to providing critical wildlife habitat and recreational opportunities like fishing and kayaking. Existing maps of watershed features can help municipalities begin to identify important water resources and the surrounding upland areas of influence on water quality and aquatic habitat.

Well-shaded, cool to coldwater streams with clean gravel substrate are able to support native fish such as brook trout that are sensitive to warmer temperatures and sedimentation. Known trout streams shown here are from the trout and trout spawning designations in NYS Department of Environmental Conservation’s water quality classification data. Some streams support migratory routes for fish traveling between ocean and freshwater habitats, such as American eel and river herring, shown in the Estuary tab. In-stream barriers, such as damsand poorly designed and installedculverts, can isolate and severely limit the range of aquatic species, decrease available habitat, increase local flooding, and lower water quality.

In addition to in-stream habitats, such as riffles, runs, and pools, streams are inherently interconnected with the upland habitats of their streamside riparian areas and floodplains, low-lying areas that are inundated during heavy rainfall or snowmelt events. Forested riparian corridors and floodplains minimize runoff and help to slow and store floodwaters. The Federal Emergency Management Agency has mapped approximate floodplains based on flood frequency according to the extent of land expected to have a 1% or greater chance of being inundated in any given year, often referred to as the “100-year flood.” It is important to note that some flood-prone areas may not appear on designated floodplain maps, and floodplain designations may change over time as more information becomes available. Note that FEMA floodplain maps shown here may be incomplete for certain areas, including Columbia and Rensselaer counties.

As development occurs in a watershed, pavement and other impervious surfaces (e.g., roofs) increase runoff volume and velocity, leading to more frequent and damaging floods. Stormwater runoff often carries chemicals, sediment, and other contaminants into streams and waterbodies, threatening water quality and degrading habitat value. Preserving forest cover and minimizing impervious surfaces in the watershed, especially in floodplains and riparian corridors, is ever more important as uplands are developed and as the frequency and magnitude of flood events increase with climate change. The average impervious surface and forest cover are displayed in the popup boxes for each watershed.

NY: NHP Natural Communities

Type:

Web Map

Tags:

LandScope; Ecosystems; NY

Snippet:

This layer's features represent element occurrences of significant natural communities (ecological communities), as recorded in the New York Natural Heritage Program's Biodiversity Database (Biotics).

Desription:

Features represent element occurrences of significant natural communities (ecological communities), as recorded in the New York Natural Heritage Program's Biodiversity Database (Biotics). An element occurrence is one natural community type at one location. Examples of community types include deep emergent marsh, red maple-hardwood swamp, dwarf shrub bog, hemlock-northern hardwood forest, and tidal creek. Natural community occurrences in this dataset are considered significant from a statewide perspective. NY Natural Heritage documents all occurrences of community types that are rare in New York State. For more common community types, NY Natural Heritage documents occurrences where the community at that location is ranked as being of excellent or good quality, by meeting specific, documented criteria for size, undisturbed and intact condition, and quality of the surrounding landscape.

A natural community is an assemblage of interacting plant and animal populations that share a common environment; the particular assemblage of plant and animal species occurs across the landscape in areas with similar environmental conditions. Significant natural communities are rare or high-quality wetlands, forests, grasslands, ponds, streams, and other types of habitats, ecosystems, and natural areas. NY Natural Heritage tracks locations of significant natural communities because they serve as habitat for a wide range of plants and animals, both rare and common; and because community occurrences in good condition support intact ecological processes and provide ecological value and services.

Pennsylvania watersheds in which TU is conducting water quality monitoring

Desription:

Several organizations in Pennsylvania are conducting water quality testing the Marcellus Shale region.  One such group is Trout Unlimited (TU).  Per TU's Pennsylvania Marcellus Shale Field Organizer Mitchell Blake:

Trout Unlimited’s Coldwater Conservation Corps (CCC) is a network of volunteer stream stewards who monitor water quality throughout the Commonwealth of Pennsylvania.  With over 350 members trained, hailing from almost every Pennsylvania Trout Unlimited chapter, the CCC volunteers focus on achieving early detection of pollution events during oil and gas drilling and production activities and collecting a baseline inventory of data on important coldwater fisheries.  Using a scientific tool that focuses on trout populations and forecasted Marcellus development, CCC volunteers strategically choose their monitoring locations within their chapter and report and map their data using Trout Unlimited’s water quality monitoring database.  Protection of coldwater fisheries is an integral part of Trout Unlimited’s mission and more than any other segment of society, it is fisherman who spend considerable time on these streams, and thus are well positioned to watch over them.

In the coming months, there are plans to share the water quality data with those who are interested.

Registered Water Withdrawals in New York State

Type:

Web Map

Tags:

water withdrawal; use; NYS; New York

Snippet:

This map shows the registered water withdrawals in New York State. Data were obtained by a Freedom of Information Law request from New York State DEC in May, 2013

Desription:

This is a map of registered water users in New York State in 2013 compiled from data provided by the NYS DEC. Only the very largest of these water users are required to apply this year for water withdrawal permits under New York's new water withdrawal permitting regulations, 6 NYCRR Parts 601 and 621,http://www.dec.ny.gov/regulations/78258.html,  which became effective April 1, 2013.   Under the new regulations, the largest users are to be issued permits first.  Section 601.7(2) of the regulations lists the dates by which a complete application for an initial permit must be submitted. These dates are shown in Table 1. Users who are required under the regulations to apply in 2013 are "systems that withdraw or are designed to withdraw a volume of 100 million gallons per day (mgd) or more," unless exempt from the permitting requirements.  Registered users withdrawing 100 mgd or more are listed in Table 2. Their applications were due June 1st, unless they are exempt from the permit requirements. Exemptions are listed in Section 601.9 of the regulations.  The staggered implementation schedule being used by the DEC makes it virtually impossible for the DEC to weigh competing usage requirements as they issue withdrawal permits. The schedule as designed gives priority for permits to the state's largest water users first, in descending order.
